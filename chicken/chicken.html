Terminology

an adult male is a called a cock or (in the United States) a rooster and an adult female is called a hen.[10][11] Other terms are:

  • Biddy: a newly hatched chicken[12][13]
  • apon: a castrated or neutered male chicken[a]

  • Chick: a young chicken[14]
  • Chook /tʃʊk/: a chicken (Australia/New Zealand, informal)[15]
  • Cockerel: a young male chicken less than a year old[16]
  • Dunghill fowl: a chicken with mixed parentage from different domestic varieties.[17]
  • Pullet: a young female chicken less than a year old.[18] In the poultry industry, a pullet is a sexually immature chicken less than 22 weeks of age.[19],
  • Yardbird: a chicken (southern United States, dialectal)[20]
  • Chicken may also mean a chick (see for example Hen and Chicken Islands).[21] In fact, chicken was originally a term only for an immature, or at least young, bird.[when?] In older sources, chicken as a species were typically referred to as common fowl or domestic fowl.[22][page needed][failed verification]

In Australian or New Zealand vernacular English the word chook provides the generic term for the species (e.g. "a cooked chook" or "she keeps chooks"); which enables chicken to commonly retain its original sense of a young or recently hatched bird. Chick is then rarely used to mean chicken, but is mainly used in Merriam-Webster's "Sense 1b" viz. the young of any bird.
